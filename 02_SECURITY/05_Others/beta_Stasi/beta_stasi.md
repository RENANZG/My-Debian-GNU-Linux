Surviving under the scrutiny of highly oppressive regimes.

### General Principles

1. **Blend In**: Avoid attracting attention. Dress, act, and speak in ways that do not stand out. Conformity can be a shield in oppressive environments.

2. **Minimalism**: Carry minimal personal information and items. The less you have on you, the less can be used against you if you are stopped or searched.

3. **Routine Variation**: Avoid predictable patterns in your daily activities. Regularly change your routes, schedules, and habits to make it harder for surveillance to track you.

### Communication and Information Security

1. **Secure Communications**: Use encrypted communication methods and be cautious about what you say and to whom. Assume that all unencrypted communications can be intercepted.

2. **Code Words and Signals**: Develop a system of code words and signals with trusted contacts to convey important messages discreetly.

3. **Destroy Sensitive Material**: Regularly destroy any written or electronic information that could be incriminating. Use methods that ensure complete destruction, such as shredding paper documents and securely wiping digital data.

### Building a Trust Network

1. **Vet Contacts Carefully**: Only trust those who have proven their reliability over time. Even then, compartmentalize information so no single person knows everything.

2. **Secure Meeting Places**: Use safe houses or secure locations for sensitive meetings. Ensure these places are not under surveillance.

3. **Anonymous Interactions**: When possible, conduct interactions anonymously or through intermediaries to protect your identity.

### Behavioral Tactics

1. **Feign Ignorance**: If questioned, act ignorant or indifferent. Expressing strong opinions or showing too much knowledge can raise suspicion.

2. **Emergency Plans**: Have a plan for what to do if you are arrested or if your home is raided. This might include having a cover story, knowing your rights, and having emergency contacts.

3. **Non-Verbal Cues**: Develop non-verbal communication methods, such as hand signals or coded gestures, for use in situations where speaking is risky.

### Physical Security

1. **Safe Storage**: Store important documents and items in secure locations. Use hiding places that are not obvious, such as false bottoms in furniture or hidden compartments.

2. **Escape Routes**: Know multiple escape routes from your home and places you frequently visit. Practice using these routes regularly.

3. **Counter-Surveillance**: Learn basic counter-surveillance techniques to identify if you are being followed and how to lose a tail.

### Psychological Preparedness

1. **Mental Resilience**: Build mental and emotional resilience to withstand stress and fear. This might include meditation, mindfulness, or other stress-reduction techniques.

2. **Support Systems**: Maintain connections with trusted friends, family, or support groups who can provide emotional support and practical help.

3. **Stay Informed**: Keep abreast of the political climate and the actions of the regime. Knowledge of current events can help you anticipate and avoid trouble.

### Legal Awareness

1. **Know Your Rights**: Familiarize yourself with the laws and your rights, even if they are limited. This knowledge can help you navigate interactions with authorities more effectively.

2. **Legal Preparedness**: Have access to a lawyer who can provide legal advice and assistance if you are detained or arrested.

### Example Historical Practices

1. **Safe Houses**: Resistance groups often used safe houses for meetings and to hide individuals from authorities. These locations were kept secret and frequently changed to avoid detection.

2. **Dead Drops**: Spies and resistance members used dead drops—hidden locations where items could be left and retrieved without direct contact between individuals.

3. **False Identities**: Using fake IDs and adopting false personas were common practices to evade detection and arrest.

4. **Radio Codes**: During World War II, resistance groups used coded radio transmissions to communicate with allied forces, ensuring that even if the messages were intercepted, the content remained secure.
